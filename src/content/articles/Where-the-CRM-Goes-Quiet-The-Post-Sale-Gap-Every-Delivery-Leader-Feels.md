CRMs are built to support the sales process: pipeline stages, forecast rollups, opportunity scoring, and contact histories all focus on one commercial milestone: closed-won. But for implementation-heavy SaaS, that milestone is not the endpoint. It is the beginning of the delivery obligation.

After the deal closes, the commitments made during the sales cycle must be fulfilled across implementation, support, and customer success. Yet the system that aligned the organization before the sale often provides limited visibility into whether those commitments are being delivered.

For post-sale leaders, this gap is familiar: the CRM captures the opportunity, but not the operating reality that determines customer confidence, renewal readiness, and long-term value.

## Where CRM stops

Modern CRMs such as Salesforce and HubSpot are excellent at managing pipeline, contacts, opportunities, and forecasting. Their workflows are designed to move deals forward and predict revenue. The issue is scope: most of what determines whether that revenue renews happens after the opportunity closes.

Implementation runs in a project tracker. Support lives in a ticketing system. Customer success operates from a health-score platform. The sales-to-delivery handoff may be captured in a meeting, a message thread, or a representative’s memory. Each team holds part of the truth, but no system holds the customer.

As a result, even a simple question — which customers are at risk right now? — becomes a search across tools, calendars, notes, and conversations.

## The gaps after closed-won

**First, the handoff is often a conversation rather than a record.** Success criteria, scope, timelines, stakeholders, and commercial context may be summarized at kickoff but rarely move cleanly into delivery. Weeks later, teams are left reconstructing what was promised.

**Second, post-sale risk is scattered.** A delayed milestone, a frustrated stakeholder, a missed training session, and a critical support issue may each appear manageable in isolation. Together, they may indicate a renewal at risk, but no system assembles the full picture.

**Third, commitments lack operational control.** Promises made during sales often live in notes or messages without clear ownership, due dates, status, or visibility. They resurface only when the customer raises them.

**Fourth, support activity is confused with support risk. **Ticket volume shows workload, not the degree to which an issue threatens adoption, go-live, confidence, or renewal.

**Finally, health scores often miss delivery reality. **Usage and engagement signals can be useful, but they may not reflect late implementations, overdue commitments, unresolved blockers, or disengaged executive sponsors.

## Why CRMs cannot simply add fields to solve it

The instinct to customize the CRM is understandable. Additional fields and objects can store more information, but storage is not the same as operating discipline.

Post-sale work has a different structure from sales. It involves handoff review, implementation milestones, readiness gates, commitment aging, support impact, renewal exposure, and cross-functional ownership. These are not merely data points attached to an opportunity; they are workflows, states, and relationships.

Similarly, project trackers manage tasks, support tools manage tickets, and customer success platforms manage motions and adoption. Each tool is valuable within its lane, but none provides a complete operating layer for the post-sale customer relationship.

The gap is therefore not a missing CRM feature. It is a missing layer between systems.

## How Event Horizon closes the gap

Event Horizon is a post-sale operating layer for implementation-heavy SaaS. It connects the white space between CRM, project management, support, and customer success without replacing the CRM.

**It makes the sales-to-delivery handoff a required workflow.** At closed-won, commitments, success criteria, scope, stakeholders, and commercial context are captured in a structured handoff record that delivery can review, accept, return, or clarify before kickoff.

**It creates one operating picture per customer. **Accounts, handoffs, implementations, commitments, support impact, and renewal readiness are connected in a shared view, so risk is visible without investigation.

**It gives commitments operational control. **Every promise has an owner, due date, status, and link to renewal impact.

**It distinguishes support impact from support volume.** Issues are connected to accounts, implementations, and renewal dates, separating ordinary queue activity from problems that threaten adoption or go-live.

**It grounds health and renewal readiness in delivery evidence.** Implementation status, open risks, overdue commitments, engagement, and value realization surface at-risk customers earlier and with supporting context.

**It gives leaders an executive portfolio view.** Teams can see implementation health, blocked work, overdue commitments, renewal exposure, and next actions across the book of business.

Sales can continue selling in Salesforce or HubSpot, while the post-sale organization gains a system designed for the work that begins after closed-won.

## What changes for post-sale leaders

Moving from reactive status-hunting to operating discipline. Instead of assembling customer risk from disconnected updates, leaders can see the evidence behind delivery confidence and act while there is still time. Handoffs become records, commitments become managed obligations, and health reflects operational reality.

For implementation-heavy SaaS, delivering what was promised at scale is what compounds into retention and expansion. It deserves a system of its own.
