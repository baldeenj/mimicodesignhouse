Product architecture is a discipline without a fixed definition. Depending on where you work, a product architect might look like a senior designer who also writes technical specifications, a product manager with deep UX expertise, or a solutions architect who can run a user research session. In my case, it means being the person responsible for how all the pieces fit together — design, technology, business requirements, and the human beings who will ultimately use what we build.

I've spent years in this space, working on products for financial institutions, airlines, and government agencies. Here's what I've learned about what the work actually requires.

## The Core Responsibility: Coherence

The most important thing a product architect does is maintain coherence. Complex products have many stakeholders, many features, many constraints, and many teams contributing to the work. Without someone whose job it is to hold the whole picture, products become incoherent — internally inconsistent, confusing to users, and expensive to maintain.

Coherence means that design decisions make sense in context. A button label that works in isolation might be confusing when users have been through six prior screens of a flow. A feature that seems simple might interact unexpectedly with another. An architectural choice that optimizes one user journey might create friction for another.

Maintaining coherence requires being involved at every stage, from initial concept through delivery, and having enough breadth to notice when something doesn't fit.

## What the Role Actually Demands

**Systems thinking:** Products are systems. User journeys connect to data models connect to business processes connect to the mental models users bring from other contexts. A product architect needs to hold all of these levels simultaneously and understand how changes at one level propagate through others.

**Fluency across disciplines:** You don't need to be the best designer, the best developer, or the best product manager on the team. You do need to be fluent enough in each discipline to evaluate trade-offs, translate between specialists, and recognize when domain expertise is being applied well versus poorly.

**Tolerance for ambiguity:** The most important questions in product architecture rarely have clear answers. What problem are we actually solving? What does success look like in three years? What are we explicitly not doing? Learning to work productively in the presence of ambiguity, and to make decisions that remain coherent even as the situation evolves, is the core skill.

**Communication as craft:** A product architecture that exists only in one person's head isn't an architecture — it's a risk. The work of product architecture includes making the thinking legible: through documentation, diagrams, facilitated workshops, and the patient work of explaining decisions and earning buy-in.

## On Making Things Real

The most satisfying part of this work is watching ideas become real — not as I originally imagined them, but as they evolve through the collision with constraints, with user research, with technical realities, with organizational politics.

That evolution isn't a failure of the original vision. It's the work. Good product architecture sets a direction and principles that survive contact with reality, while remaining flexible enough to incorporate what reality teaches.

The ideas that matter most are the ones that survive the journey from concept to something a real person can use to accomplish something real in their life. That's what I'm trying to build.
