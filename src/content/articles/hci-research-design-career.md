My path into design ran through HCI research, which is not the most common route. Most designers come through visual arts, graphic design, architecture, or industrial design. I came through studying how people interact with technology — the cognitive, social, and physiological dimensions of human-computer interaction.

That background shaped everything about how I approach design problems. It's not just that I know research methods; it's that I have a particular view of what design is for, and what good evidence looks like.

## What HCI Research Actually Is

HCI is an interdisciplinary field sitting at the intersection of computer science, cognitive psychology, ergonomics, and design. It's concerned with understanding how people interact with computing systems — what makes interfaces usable, what cognitive models users bring to digital products, how context and environment affect interaction, and how to design systems that fit human capabilities and limitations.

Research in HCI uses a mix of methods borrowed from these parent disciplines: controlled experiments (where treatment and control can be isolated), qualitative studies (contextual inquiry, think-aloud protocols, diary studies), physiological measurement (eye tracking, biometric data), and increasingly, large-scale behavioral data analysis.

The thing these methods share is a commitment to grounding claims in evidence — not intuition, not convention, not what seems obvious to the designer.

## What Research Training Changed About How I See Design

Before HCI research training, I thought design was fundamentally about making things that look right and feel right to the designer. After it, I understood that design is about making things that work for people who aren't the designer — and that the gap between "feels right to me" and "works for this user" is reliably wider than it appears.

Research training builds specific skills:

**Separating observation from interpretation.** When watching someone use a product, novice observers jump immediately to conclusions. Trained researchers try to stay in observation — what exactly did the person do, say, hesitate at — before forming hypotheses about why. This distinction is the foundation of useful research.

**Recognizing the limits of self-report.** What people say they do and what they actually do are different, often predictably so. Observational research closes this gap; interview-only research can't.

**Forming falsifiable hypotheses.** Good research design requires specifying in advance what evidence would change your belief. This discipline makes it possible to actually learn from research rather than just confirming existing assumptions.

**Contextual analysis.** Behavior in a usability lab looks different from behavior in context. Research training builds awareness of ecological validity — whether your findings will hold outside your study conditions.

## Applying This in Commercial Design Practice

HCI research principles translate directly to design practice, even when you're not running formal studies:

**In discovery:** The discipline of observing before interpreting makes you a better listener in stakeholder interviews and user research sessions. You get more accurate information when you're genuinely curious rather than leading.

**In evaluation:** When reviewing your own design work, HCI training makes you ask "by what evidence would I know this is working?" — a question that keeps you from falling in love with your own solutions.

**In synthesis:** HCI research produces a lot of data that has to be analyzed and interpreted. Pattern recognition across research sessions, separating signal from noise, and constructing models that accurately represent diverse users are all skills that transfer to design synthesis.

**In communication:** Designers who can connect their decisions to evidence — observations, behavioral data, prior research — are more credible with engineers, product managers, and executives who think in terms of evidence.

## What HCI Research Can't Give You

Research training is not design training. Understanding how people interact with interfaces doesn't automatically translate into designing better ones. Diagnosis and treatment are different skills.

What HCI research gave me was a set of heuristics for what good evidence looks like, and a deep respect for the gap between designer mental models and user mental models. I still need design craft, visual judgment, and creative problem-solving to translate that understanding into solutions.

But I wouldn't trade the research foundation. It keeps me honest about what I know versus what I assume — which is maybe the most important thing a designer can be.
